Head Rub

February & March 2026

Build Log: Head Rub · iOS · February & March 2026
iOS · React Native · Launched

Since November of 2025, I've been a somewhat regular user of a simple app called Bend. Bend audibly guides users through simple stretching routines of varying length, and is reportedly earning $500,000 a month in subscription revenue.

I'm not typically motivated by the financial potential of a project, but had recently created a note in my phone of all the pressure points and head massage techniques that were most effective at relieving my migraines.

Head Rub is a mobile application designed to guide folks experiencing migraines, eye strain, or other tension in their head, face, and neck through self-administered massage routines. The graphics are hand drawn by me, and audio voice overs provided by my Dad.

Lessons

01

Database-driven development, with a regimented build plan.

In February, I participated in the "28 Days of Lovable" challenge, where Lazar Jovanovic walked vibe coders through his preferred method for building products. Despite not using Lovable or building Head Rub as a web app, I found his ideas on executing build plans, building database-first, and context management particularly useful.

Instead of re-writing his work, I'll simply link to the best content in the month:

  • Database Thinking (This Is What Makes Apps Real)
  • Documenting Backwards (This Is How Projects Stay Alive)
  • How I Actually Build (Systems Beat Memory)

02

Distribution is the new highest hurdle to jump.

According to Sensor Tower, new app submissions to the App Store jumped 84% in the first quarter of 2026 compared to the same period in 2025. The media primarily reported this as evidence that the App Store was "filling with AI vibe coded slop," but I read it as a growing discoverability and distribution challenge.

This pushed me to focus even more narrowly on migraine sufferers. By keeping my audience narrowly targeted, I felt that I could leverage both my personal story and uncommon channels for distribution.

03

Design a lifestyle, not a business.

Silicon Valley has long reinforced that "Ideas are cheap, execution is what matters." But what happens in a world where execution is also cheap? For a creative and curious person, deciding what to build becomes a more nuanced question.

When making that choice, I considered questions like: Who do I want to serve? Who would I love to do user research with? What do I miss getting to do in my daily life? Who would I love to collaborate with? What social issues would I love to support?

The questions revealed two places where my Head Rub app idea uniquely had potential: I missed building projects with my Dad, and I missed getting to draw.

Drawing by hand

Like many product designers, I was feeling legitimately unmoored by the shift in my role. The "making" part of my work is increasingly outsourced to AI, meaning the task work I loved most — disappearing into a Figma flow-state for an afternoon — was gone.

Instead of drawing to design, increasingly designers are expected to write to do our work. This shift has opened the door to a much wider set of people now able to design products, which is something I believe to be unequivocally a good thing.

But the difference between drawing and writing feels, to me, as substantial as the difference between talking and singing. They may achieve the same end, but the way I feel isn't the same. And I really missed drawing.

With Head Rub, I found a concept that was going to require lots of graphics that referenced hands, heads, musculature and anatomy — images that AI specifically struggles to accurately generate. Getting to hand draw these graphics scratched an itch that my day job no longer provided, and acts as a kind of defensive moat for the app preventing others from building a competitive product quickly.

The voice of a concerned Dad

In the age of AI, it's easy to generate a fake human voice. But Head Rub is a product that someone experiencing a migraine uses when they're in pain, nauseous, and alone in the dark. (Speaking from personal experience.)

When I thought of who might provide the voice for the product, I thought of my Dad and his "radio voice." I remembered the way he talked to my little sister in recovery rooms after her medical procedures, or my brother after he lost a baseball game, or talked to me on the front porch swing during thunder storms. He's able to drop into a calming register that helps folks relax when they're stressed. Head Rub needed the voice of a concerned Dad.

And it gave me an excuse to call my Dad more frequently, and collaborate on a project that leveraged both of our skills.

In both cases, I solved for something that was lacking in my life, in a way that added unique value to the project.
