Build Log: BRB · iOS · February 2026
iOS · React Native · TestFlight

I'm not great at keeping up with my friends. As I get older and more friends start families or move to new cities, I sometimes let folks I care about "fall out of orbit" as our rhythm of connection shifts.

I wanted to build a lightweight tool that could help me build a habit of staying in touch, and be more intentional about nurturing my friendships.

Leaning on the best practices in habit forming and social science, I built BRB — a nostalgic AIM-style app targeted at millennials that suggests one person per day to reach out to for 30 days. My hope was that with a defined goal, timely reminders, and some social pressure, people that used the app would re-engage old friendships and feel more connected to their friends.

Lessons

01

Prototype the riskiest part first.

With this build, I spent significantly more time discussing the plan with AI, strategizing around the MVP, documenting the build plan, and describing the behavior change I wanted the product to support.

I discovered that the hardest part of the build would be the mechanism that chose a random name from a user-generated contact list once per day, tracked who was contacted versus skipped, and appropriately shifted from random selection to targeted selection at a specific day in the 30-day challenge when the number of skipped names was greater than days left in the challenge. So I prototyped it, and built a tool that allowed me to simulate a months-long challenge in 30 minutes instead of 30 days.

Prototyping the riskiest, most complex part of the app first was helpful. It clarified my thinking, and confirmed that the AI was capable of delivering the experience I imagined.

02

Throw away the dang prototype.

The costly mistake I made in this build was then trying to build the rest of the production app around that prototype. The deeper into the build I got, the more I was tangled in my own simulator tooling, throwaway styling, and wasting tokens building screens that would have been included in my foundational prompt.

03

Include the design system in your first, foundational prompt.

With BRB, I defined a design system file including fonts, colors, typography, and spacing after prototyping the core interaction of the app in a throwaway style. In future projects, I learned to pass in design guardrails from the first prompts, ensuring consistency and giving me the ability to precisely describe adjustments instead of making arbitrary refinements screen by screen. (i.e. Prompts like "Update to our label treatment" instead of "Make this text smaller and lighter.")

04

Vibecoding can feel a lot like gambling.

A gambler's dream is to become rich, which is oftentime a simple way of saying "become free." Free from the need to find a job, deal with a commute, an overbearing boss, or the fear of not having enough. Entrepreneurship shares this dream, and vibecoding can make that kind of entrepreneurial freedom feel like it's always just one more prompt away.

Garry Tan, President of YCombinator, and other leaders in tech described this feeling as "cyber psychosis," and laugh through podcasts encouraging young technology workers to completely ignore how much they spend on tokens and sleep under their desks. They romanticize the feeling of obsession with very little caution.

With the BRB build, I found myself falling into the same variable reward mechanism as a slot machine or infinite scroll. I really felt the emotional swings of it working some days, and things feeling like a tangled mess on others.

I came out of the experience with a stronger conviction that vibe coding, for most people, is entertainment. And the entertainment it provides was more like a casino, crypto, or prediction markets than catching a movie with your friends. It's a tool technology workers like myself need to learn how to wield in healthy ways, and industry leaders should be describing how to achieve a healthy relationship with AI.

I'm still figuring out what a healthy relationship with it looks like, but I think naming it honestly is a start.

05

User research still matters.

After building BRB, I sent it to 17 friends who had generously offered to try it out. Friends don't necessarily make the best testers given their somewhat compromised motivations, but data doesn't lie. I found that the majority of my testers stopped using the app daily within 6 or 7 days.

This was tough to swallow, but renewed my faith in the need to stay brutally focused on getting an MVP into customer hands.
